Breaking News — Rome in Crisis

CAESAR SLAIN
IN THE SENATE

Roman General Struck Down by 23 Conspirators at the Base of Pompey's Statue; Brutus Among the Assassins; City Descends Into Shock and Fury

By Our Senate Correspondent  ·  Staff of the Roman Post

La morte di Cesare — Vincenzo Camuccini, 1804–1805. Oil on canvas. Museo Nazionale di Capodimonte, Naples. (Mark Antony's Oration at Caesar's Funeral)

ROME, THE THEATRE OF POMPEY —

Julius Caesar, Dictator of Rome and the most powerful man in the known world, was assassinated this morning in the Theatre of Pompey during a session of the Senate. He was 55 years of age. A group of approximately 23 senators, who have since named themselves the Liberatores — the Liberators — surrounded Caesar at the base of Pompey's statue and stabbed him in a coordinated attack. Physicians on the scene report that Caesar sustained 23 separate wounds, though only one proved fatal.

The conspiracy was masterminded by Gaius Cassius Longinus, a former general who had long harboured resentment toward Caesar's rise to power.

Upon recognising Brutus among his attackers, Caesar ceased to resist and addressed Brutues as he cried out "Et tu, Brutè?" while he fell.

Most shocking to the Roman public was the involvement of Marcus Junius Brutus — Caesar's close friend and a man Caesar had personally pardoned after the Battle of Pharsalus. Eyewitnesses report that upon recognising Brutus among his attackers, Caesar ceased to resist, covered his face with his toga, and uttered his final words.

Caesar had been warned. Just weeks ago, a soothsayer approached him in the streets with a single warning: "Beware the Ides of March." Caesar dismissed the man as a dreamer. This very morning, his wife Calpurnia begged him to stay home, reporting nightmares of his statue running with blood and Romans bathing their hands in it.

Caesar was persuaded by senator Decius Brutus, who told him the Senate planned to offer him the crown today — and that refusing to attend would appear cowardly. He left for the Senate and never returned.

Mark Antony, Caesar's most loyal general and co-consul, was deliberately kept outside the Senate chamber by the conspirator Trebonius during the attack. Had Antony been present, those close to the situation believe the outcome may have been very different. Antony has since retrieved Caesar's body and is expected to speak at the funeral.

Rome is in shock. Citizens have gathered in the Forum in enormous numbers. The conspirators appeared on the steps of the Capitol shortly after the killing, calling out that Rome was free — but were met not with celebration, but with fear and confusion.

As of this evening, Brutus and Cassius have retreated to their homes, and armed guards have been posted throughout the city. The future of the Republic is deeply uncertain.

MARCUS LEPIDUS Offers his legions in service to the stability of Rome. The Republic must be defended. Report to the Aventine Hill.

Inside Rome — Funeral & Aftermath

The Forum Romanum

THE CITY AGAINSANTONY TURNS T THE CONSPIRATORS IN STUNNING FUNERAL ORATION

General's Speech Calls Brutus "Honorable" So Many Times the Word Becomes an Accusation; Will Reveals Caesar Left Money to Every Citizen

By Our Forum Correspondent


What began as a measured funeral oration became perhaps the most consequential speech ever delivered in the Forum Romanum. Mark Antony, given permission by the conspirators to speak at Caesar's funeral on the condition that he not blame them directly, proceeded to dismantle their reputation word by word.

Antony opened by insisting he came only to bury Caesar, not to praise him — but over the course of his speech, he systematically dismantled every justification the conspirators had offered. He referred to Brutus repeatedly as "an honorable man," a phrase he repeated so many times that by the end, the crowd understood it as the deepest possible insult.

He displayed Caesar's bloodied toga to the crowd, pointing out each tear made by a different conspirator's blade. He named Brutus's stab wound the "most unkindest cut of all," arguing that it was Brutus's betrayal — not the physical wound — that truly killed Caesar.

Then Antony produced Caesar's will. Caesar had left 75 drachmas to every Roman citizen, and his personal gardens along the Tiber to the public as a park for all people to use freely. The reading of the will sent the crowd into a frenzy of grief and rage. Citizens tore apart wooden benches and market stalls to build a funeral pyre for Caesar on the spot.

Mobs then turned on the homes of the conspirators, forcing Brutus and Cassius to flee Rome entirely. Political analysts note that Antony never once directly accused the conspirators of wrongdoing — yet the effect of his speech was more devastating than any accusation could have been.

"He used Caesar's own generosity as a weapon, and let the crowd's emotion do the rest."

Rome — Power Struggle

Cinna the Poet releases horrible poem

Not-Well known poet, Cinna, has published his latest poem. Critics are calling it "disturbing" and "the worst peace of literature" they have ever read.

By Our Literary Correspondent


As Rome celebrates the Feast of Lupercal, various forms of entertainment and celebration are taking place throughout the city. Poet Gaius Helvius Cinna decided to debut his newly written poem titled "Lupo et Luna" (Wolf and the Moon) which was met with overwhelmingly negative reviews.

Quoted as the "Worst tragedy in Roman history" has overshadowed the Feast of Lupercal where the Luperci priests run through the streets naked and whip anyone within striking distance with februa*, thongs made from newly sacraficed goats. the Witnesses of this event report that the poem was filled with disturbing imagery and language that many found offensive. "It was poor in taste," said one critic. "It was a disgrace to the art of poetry."

Even Rome's most esteemed poets have expressed their disapproval. "This is not literature, its so bad it should be considered a crime" said one. Cinna has responded to this criticism by insisting that the poem was "misunderstood by an audience without sophistaction," claiming that Roman General Julius Caesar was one of the few who truly appreciated his work as General Caesar appeared to smile after its recital. Reports say that Cinna is planning to publish a follow-up poem to address the criticism.

Update as of the Ides of March, XLIV BCE: This is now known as the second worst tragedy of the year.

*The word "februa" is derived from the Latin word "februum," meaning purification. The februa were used in the Lupercalia festival as a symbolic tool for cleansing and fertility rites. (Pretty cool fact if you ask me)

Editorial — The View of the Post

DID ROME TRADE ONE TYRANT FOR ANOTHER?


The men who killed Julius Caesar called themselves the liberators of Rome, but have they taken too much liberty with Rome’s future? (Pun) They explain that their actions are justified as Caesar's ambition posed a threat to the Roman Republic. Because of this, Brutus had been praised as a person who loved Rome more than this closest friends.

But the question remains: What exactly has been liberated?

Rome is outraged tonight, the very same rite where Brutus addresed the poeple and asked for their understanding had Antony revert the feeling of hatreed. The hunt for the conspirators has begun, homes pillaged and burned. Senior senators of Rome are not fugitives in their own republic.

Brutus admits that Caesar had not yet committed the crimes that justified his death, the assassination was a preemptive strike against potential tyranny. However, this is a dangerous principle. If a man may be killed for for his shadow's doing (Metaphor), then no one is safe in Rome

The conspirators were not wrong about the threat that corruption poses. But they were horrendously wrong about the fix. They removed one man and a void has been left behind. History will have to decide whether Brutus was a hero or a villain , but Rome's future generations will pay the price forwhat has occurred.

Obituaries — Those Claimed by the Ides (And actions that followed)

Julius Caesar

100 BCE – 15 March, 44 BCE  ·  Aged 55  ·  Dictator of Rome

Gaius Julius Caesar, age 55, Rome's greatest general, was killed yesterday in the Senate House by a group of conspirators that claim to have acted in the Republic's best interest. The city has erupted in grief and anger following the funeral rites held this afternoon.

Caesar, celebrated for his military achievements including his victories in Gaul. His popularity among the common people was unmatched, earning him widespread devotion. However, these honors have been announced as a threat to our democracy by Marcus Brutus, one of the conspirators. The assassination was carried out in the Senate where witnesses have reported that Caesar recived multiple stabs on the back. Falling to the ground as he uttered his final words.

Caesar is survived by his wife, Calpurnia, and his son, Octavius, who is expected to return to Rome soon. The political situation for Rome continues to be uncertain as crowds begin to rally in demand for retaliation against the conspirators.

Cinna The Poet

90 BCE – 44 BCE  ·  Poet

Gaius Cinna, the renowned poet, was killed today in the streets of Rome. He was attacked by a mob of angry citizens who mistakingly belived he was involved in the assassination of Caesar.

While making his way to attend Caesar's funeral rites, he was confronted by citizens who asked him to identify himself. Despite clarifying his identity and his profession as a poet, he was still attacked and killed.

Witnesses have reported that the mob's agitation was fueled by Mark Antony's speech which stirred the people to anger towards who killed Caesar. However, it has also been reported that many people recognized him from his performance of his poem at the celebration for the Feast of Lupercal. "Tear him for his bad verses, tear him for his bad verses!" one of the Plebians shouted.

Portia

70 BCE – 44 BCE  ·  Wife of Brutus, Daughter of Cato

The daughter of the great Stoic senator Cato the Younger (Marcus Porcius Cato), Portia was in every sense her father's daughter: Brave and Unwilling to accept any treatment she considered beneath her dignity. When she sensed that her husband Brutus was hidingsomething of great importance, she did not hesitate to stab her own thigh to prove to him that she could bear pain and keep secrets.

It worked, Brutus told her everything. When news came that Brutus had fled Rome and that the cause was lost, Portia committed suicide, reportedly by swallowing burning coals. A death both horrible and, in its Roman stubbornness, a demonstration of her strength and principles. (Just like her father)
